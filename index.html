Mark Roseman

Pat M Glazer Chair, Jewish Studies Program
Professor, Department of History
Adjunct Professor, Department of German

I am a historian of modern Europe, with particular interests in the History of the Holocaust and in modern German history. My publications have covered a wide range of topics in German, European and Jewish history, including life-reform and protest in 1920s and 1930s Germany; Holocaust survival and memory; Nazi policy and perpetrators; the social impact of total war; post-1945 German and European reconstruction; generation conflict and youth rebellion; Jewish and other minorities in modern German history.

I also have an interest in comparative history and in particular in German-Japanese comparisons. My current research projects include a critical synthesis of recent work on Nazi perpetrators, and a project looking at a life-reform and resistance group in Germany 1920-2000.

Teaching Philosophy


I have colleagues who dream of being able to leave the classroom to spend all their time on research. For myself, I cannot imagine this. It is not just that I have always understood myself as a teacher as well as a researcher. More fundamentally, the dialog between teaching and research is central to both. Above all, I believe that it is teaching that keeps you “real”. Particularly for a historiography like that of Nazism and the Holocaust that has made so many turns, the need to present a coherent picture to even a well-read and highly sophisticated student group is an essential, recurring reality check. And sometimes, a classroom discussion ushers in a real breakthrough in one’s own thinking.

I am committed to active learning, and all my lectures expressly include student participation, moments of reflection and conversation between students and so forth. My most rewarding classes are the smaller intensive writing seminars and capstone courses. In these classes I adopt a process of scaffolding, gradually building up the student skill set in preparation for writing a longer paper, and using a variety of techniques, including peer review, to foster an active classroom. My students have been extremely successful at winning prizes in the University, and I have more than half a dozen students who won the European History Prize, the Jewish Studies essay prize, or the Library’s Sam Burgess research award.

Since coming to Indiana University, my teaching has been enriched by being closely connected with the Scholarship on Teaching initiatives developed by David Pace. I took part in their seminars, and worked with IU’s Center for Innovative Learning and Teaching to have myself observed and filmed in the lecture hall. The seminars increased my awareness of the importance of active learning, and of building in responsive exercises, small group discussion and so forth, into even a large lecture. As a consequence of this interaction, I was interviewed by Alan Booth for the project “Historians on Teaching” which can be found here. While I was Director of Undergraduate Studies for the History Department, I also introduced some of the first departmental seminars designed explicitly to talk about teaching. More recently I was part of a small group organized by IU’s Center for Innovative Teaching & Learning to workshop John Bean’s Engaging Ideas.

A good example of my lecture style is a filmed lecture I gave to a group of high school and University students at the University of Oxford two years ago. It was an event organized around Saul Friedländer’s visiting Professorship there. The given format precluded more give and take with the audience, since the lecture was being filmed, but in terms of the tone, level, and clarity, I think this gives a good idea of my approach. The general link can be found here , but the easier route is to download the mp4 and then scroll forward 57 minutes through the seminar to where my contribution begins.
